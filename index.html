I am Rajas Chari, a graduate student at the Institute for Consensed Matter Theory(ICMT) at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign(UIUC).

Introduction

My research is in the area of Condensed Matter Theory(CMT) and Quantum computing and Information theory.

  • I am interested in topological phases of matter and their mathematical chracterization. My work involves trying to understand the topological quantum field theories(TQFTs), which describe the IR properties of these systems. I also study the classification of topological theories using methods in Category theory, Algebraic topology and Gauge theory.
  • I am also interested in the study of computational semantics of quantum algorithms, which relies on methods from category theory. Some of my notes related to this topic can be found here.

I am an active member of the quantum computing community in Chicago, where I regularly attend talks and seminars organized by the Illinois Quantum Information Science and Technology Center(IQUIST) and the Chicago Quantum Exchange(CQE). I actively engage in hackathons related to quantum computing whenever feasible. Over the course of my graduate studies, I have participated in two events, securing victory in both hackathons:

  1. The Big Q Hackathon hosted by the Chicago Quantum Exchange and QuantX, where my team won first place in the technical part of the competition. I was later interviewd by the Grainger College at UIUC, which you can find here. Notes on this project can be found in the research interests and projects section.
  2. Quantum machine learning(QML) hackathon organized by IBM Quantum and HQAN, where my team won joint first place.

Research interests and projects

  • Using Quantum annealing and Machine learning to solve optimization problems: here we review work done in the BigQ hackathon, and present the important details of the algorithm which my team proposed to solve the sphere packing problem. The algorithm is generic enough to be extended for use to other optimization problems. Notes can be found here.
  • Category theory based classification of topological phases: where we try to classify invertible topological phases by viewing topological theories as functors from the category of cobordisms to tHe category of Hilbert spaces. Notes can be found here.
  • Categorical semantics of quantum protocols: this project is based on work done by Bob Coecke in his papers which try to understand quantum algorithms from a categorical perspective. The approach has far reaching consequences, including a theoretical understanding of why quantum algorithms provide exponential speed-ups in natural language processing algorithms. Notes can be found here.

    1. Realistic version of Conway's game of life: here we simulate a version of the game of life called Lenia with digital creatures which show lifelike features like self-organization, self-repair, bilateral and radial symmetries, locomotive dynamics, and sometimes chaotic nature.
    2. Fractal's: here are some simulations which are based on Fractals and the mathematics behind fractal objects.

Publications

  1. Magnetoelectric generation of a Majorana-Fermi surface in Kitaev's honeycomb model

    Physical Review B, American Physical Society Â· Apr 1, 2021 (Show publication)

    Abstract: We study the effects of static magnetic and electric fields on Kitaev's honeycomb model. Using the electric polarization operator appropriate for Kitaev materials, we derive the effective Hamiltonian for the emergent Majorana fermions to second order in both the electric and magnetic fields. We find that while individually each perturbation does not qualitatively alter Kitaev spin liquid, the cross term induces a finite chemical potential at each Dirac node, giving rise to a Majorana-Fermi surface. We argue this gapless phase is stable and exhibits typical metallic phenomenology, such as linear in temperature heat capacity and finite, but nonquantized, thermal Hall response. Finally, we speculate on the potential for realization of this physics in Kitaev materials.

  2. Revealing the hidden Dirac gap in topological antiferromagnet using Floquet-Bloch manipulation

    Nature Physics(under review)

    Abstract: Manipulating solids using the time-periodic drive of a laser pulse is a promising route to generate new phases of matter. Whether such "Floquet-Bloch" manipulation can be achieved in topological magnetic systems with disorder has so far been unclear. In this work, we realize Floquet-Bloch manipulation of the Dirac surface-state mass of the topological antiferromagnet (AFM) MnBi2Te4. Using time- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (tr-ARPES), we show that opposite helicities of mid-infrared circularly polarized light result in substantially different Dirac mass gaps in the AFM phase, despite the equilibrium Dirac cone being massless. We explain our findings in terms of a Dirac fermion with a random mass. Our results underscore Floquet-Bloch manipulation as a powerful tool for controlling topology even in the presence of disorder, and for uncovering properties of materials that may elude conventional probes.
